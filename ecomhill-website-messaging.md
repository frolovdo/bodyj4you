# eCom Hill — Website Tone & Messaging Review

**Goal:** keep the human, friendly personality (the "apology dog" concept, the Denis + Neo
story, the calm voice) while making the pages read more like a **professional, Amazon-focused
service site** and less like an internal **job / staffing / ticket board**. Balance the
*human side* and the *professional side*.

This is a copy deck reviewing every section of the live site (mobile, ecomhill.com). Lines
marked **KEEP** already work. Lines marked **CHANGE** are the rewrites. Drop the new copy
straight into your platform (Webflow/Framer/WP/etc.).

---

## The two sections making it feel like a "job page"

Your instinct is right, and it traces to two specific sections:

### A. Pricing — "WHO YOU'RE HIRING, BY THE HOUR"  ← the biggest offender
> `$140/hr Senior Project Manager` · `$110/hr Project Manager` · `$80/hr Specialist`

This is a **staffing-agency rate card**. It frames the visitor as an *employer hiring
contractors by job title and hour* — the literal language of a job page. An Amazon seller
isn't trying to *hire a Project Manager*; they're trying to *get an ASIN un-suppressed*. The
hourly-by-role framing buries the Amazon outcome behind your internal org chart.

**Fix:** sell **outcomes and service tiers**, not "who you're hiring by the hour." Keep the
honesty (transparent pricing is a strength) but lead with what the seller *gets*. Move the
hourly detail to a small "How we bill" footnote for the people who ask.

### B. "What we fix" — the `HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW` severity pills
Each service card carries a severity tag — the visual language of a **support-ticket queue /
Jira backlog**, not a service menu. Buyers read severity as *"how big a chore is this for
them,"* not *"what do I get."*

**Fix:** replace severity pills with **turnaround + outcome**. Lead each card with the result
(revenue restored / risk removed), not the internal task name.

Everything else on the site — the dog concept, "Why small is the point," the About story — is
the *good* human side. Keep it. The job is to make the **services and pricing** carry the same
professional, Amazon-focused weight.

## 6. Pricing — reframe from "hiring staff" to "Amazon service tiers"

**Headline — CHANGE:** ~~"Honest rates. No leashes."~~ → **"Honest pricing. No retainers, no lock-in."**
Keep the transparency paragraph — it's a real differentiator — but cut the staffing framing.

### Replace "WHO YOU'RE HIRING, BY THE HOUR" entirely
Don't sell job titles by the hour. Sell **what the seller gets**. Lead with the monthly tiers
(which already exist further down) and present them as Amazon service plans:

> **CHANGE eyebrow:** ~~"WHO YOU'RE HIRING, BY THE HOUR"~~ → **"WHAT IT COSTS TO FIX YOUR HILL"**

| Tier | Rewrite (outcome-led) |
|---|---|
| **Under $1K/mo — Light support** | "A steady senior hand for brands already on Amazon — plus one-off help on a specific problem. ~8–10 hrs." |
| **$1–2K/mo — Steady management** | "Moderate management or a product launch — enough hours to run PPC and the catalog properly. ~13–20 hrs." |
| **$2–6K/mo — Full management & design** | "Advertising, listing optimization, design, and split testing — plus fixing whatever breaks: suspensions, brand registry, listings. ~20–80 hrs." |

**Move the role rates into a small footnote**, framed as transparency, not a menu:
> *How we bill:* "One person leads your account — a senior project manager or a project
> manager, never a committee — supported by specialists for PPC, content, design, and video.
> Everything is billed by the hour ($80–$140 depending on seniority), on demand: no contracts,
> no minimums, no retainer padding. We tell you the hour estimate before we start, and
> committing $4,000+/month (or a large one-time project) drops the rate."

> Why this matters: a seller scanning the page now sees *"plans that fix my Amazon problems
> for $X/month,"* not *"I'm hiring a Project Manager at $110/hr."* Same honesty, professional
> frame.

**"Sniff test" offer — KEEP**, light edit. The concept is great; just make the headline read
the value, keep the dog wit as the kicker:
